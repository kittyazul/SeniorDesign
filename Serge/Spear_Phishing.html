SPEAR PHISHING


PART I: DEFINITION


While regular phishing attacks rely on tricking unsuspecting victims into believing that the message is something they want or need, spear phishing targets a specific individual or organization. The attacker will conduct extensive research on their target in order to make the attack more personalized and increase the likelihood of the target falling into their trap. Although often intended to steal data for malicious purposes, attackers may also intend to install malware on a targeted userâ€™s computer.

On the one hand, some targeted spear phishing campaigns may involve documents containing malware or links to credential stealing sites with the goal to steal sensitive information, valuable intellectual property, or simply compromise payment systems. On the other hand, other spear phishing campaigns avoid malicious payloads in favor of advanced social engineering methods. In addition to being more complex and harder to detect, these campaigns aim to hijack processes for a small number of large payouts via a single or series of bank transfers.



PART II: HOW ARE SPEAR PHISHING ATTACKS CONDUCTED?


Along with extremely focused targeting, spear-phishing campaigns contain a large reconnaissance element. Attacker might start with emails harvested from a data breach. They will use that to gain unauthorized access into the organization's systems. Then, they will observe their target's traffic and communication. Once they have obtained enough information to conduct their attack, they will start by sending an email.

When it arrives, it will appear to have come from a trustworthy source. However, it will often lead the unsuspecting victim to a website full of malware. It is worth mentioning that spear phishing emails are harder to detect because they often use clever tactics to get victims' attention. More often than not, they will be so well-crafted that it will be very hard to spot with a naked eye.

Simply put, traditional security countermeasures will often not stop these attacks because of how cleverly customized they are. One employee's mistake or carelessness can have serious consequences on the business or organization as a whole. With that stolen data, attackers can do a pleathora of malicious activities ranging from revealing sensitive information, manipulating stock prices or simply committing various acts of espionage.

Furthermore, undetected "spear-phished" systems can deploy malware to hijack other computers. Attackers can use them to organize a botnet (enornous network of compromised systems) that can be used for denial of service attacks later on. The only way to truly fight spear phishing scams is to make employees aware of this persistent threat through training.
