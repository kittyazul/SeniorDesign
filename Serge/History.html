History of Phishing



PART I: HOW PHISHING BEGAN...


The term and concept of phishing originally began in the early 1990s via America Online, or AOL. First, it started with a group of hackers that banded together and called themselves The Warez Community. Together, they created an algorithm that allowed them to generate random credit card numbers, which they could then use to attempt to make phony AOL accounts. Whenever they would hit a match to a real card, they would create an account and spam others in the AOL’s community. Though they only needed a few to take the bait and reveal their PII such as their passwords, birth dates, credit card numbers, and/or social security numbers, the group's end goal was to use the stolen AOL accounts to send spams or launch further phishing attacks.

Though AOL eventually thwarted the group’s credit card generators in 1995, it only proved to be a minor setup back. In reality, it was already too late because by then, the group had already moved onto what has become the hallmark of phishing: impersonation of trusted organizations or individuals in order to con PII from unsuspecting victims.



PART II: HOW PHISHING HAS EVOLVED OVER THE YEARS...


In recent years, phishing attacks have been on an exponential rise. 2016 turned out to be a banner year for phishing as, according to APWG (Anti-Phishing Working Group), the total number of phishing attacks was 1,220,523. It was a 65% increase over 2015. In other words, an average of about 92,564 phishing attacks were carried out per month. That is an increase of over 5,753% over the last 12 years. Losses from phishing exploits are estimated to be as high as $5 billion in a report released in early 2014 by Microsoft. In 2019, phishing attacks reach whole new levels of creativity and sophistication as they began targeting legitimate cloud services like those offered by Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and other providers.

Simply put, phishing has become a highly-organized and even commoditized industry. Though it may be constantly evolving, it is important to remember that every phishing attempt will always expect a fast reaction out of you. So, in a way, when you are pressured online into doing something without hesitation, do the exact opposite and calmly double check the information in front of you or, consult an expert/trusted outside source when in doubt. Understanding contemporary phishing techniques will help you distinguish real emails from a fake ones, legitimate hyperlinks from covert redirects, and cloned websites from the official websites.
